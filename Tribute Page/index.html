Marie Curie: A Legacy in Science

Tracing the life of the pathbreaking physicist and chemist, 1867 – 1934.

Five Defining Milestones

2. Discovery of Radioactivity (1898)

With Pierre Curie, she isolated **Polonium** and **Radium**. Marie coined the term "radioactivity," fundamentally changing the study of matter.

3. First Nobel Prize (1903)

She became the first woman to win a Nobel Prize for Physics. This recognized her joint research on radiation phenomena with her husband, Pierre, and Henri Becquerel.

4. Second Nobel Prize (1911)

She won her second, individual Nobel Prize in Chemistry for isolating pure radium. She remains the only person honored in two different sciences.

5. Wartime X-Rays (WWI)

Curie developed and drove mobile X-ray units, or "petites Curies," to the front lines, helping to treat over a million wounded soldiers.

Scientific and Humanitarian Impact

Marie Curie's life was a testament to dedication and genius. Her work laid the foundation for modern nuclear physics and chemistry. She consistently prioritized scientific progress and humanitarian aid over personal gain, notably by refusing to patent the radium isolation process. Her legacy is not just one of groundbreaking discovery, but also of profound ethical commitment to the benefit of humanity.
